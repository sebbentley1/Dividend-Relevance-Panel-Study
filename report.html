Overview

What This Project Is

This is a self-directed empirical project built during my second year of a BSc Economics and Finance degree. The goal was to apply panel econometric methods to a real financial question — specifically, whether changes in a firm's dividend policy have a measurable effect on its market valuation.

The theoretical anchor is the Modigliani-Miller (1961) dividend irrelevance proposition, which argues that in frictionless markets, firm value is determined by earnings power and investment decisions, not by how those earnings are distributed to shareholders. This remains one of the most debated propositions in corporate finance, and I wanted to test it empirically rather than just describe it.

The project went through two iterations. The first version used only yfinance for data collection and had three methodological weaknesses I identified and corrected in the version documented here.

Data Sources

Data Sources — and an Honest Note on Their Limits

Fundamental data (income statements, balance sheets, cash flows) for 15 firms was sourced from the SimFin bulk API, which provides clean, standardised annual financials. The remaining 10 firms — primarily UK-headquartered stocks and US banks not covered by SimFin's free tier — were sourced via the yfinance API using the same ratio construction methodology.

Data Limitation

The hybrid sourcing approach introduces a potential consistency risk: SimFin and yfinance may apply different accounting adjustments, particularly for non-US firms reporting under IFRS. I do not believe this materially affects the results — both sources pull from SEC/exchange filings — but it is a limitation a more rigorous study would address by using a single institutional data provider such as Compustat or Bloomberg.

Why Log Total Assets as the Dependent Variable?

Log Assets is a measure of firm size, not firm value. The theoretically correct dependent variable for a dividend relevance test is Tobin's Q (market value of assets / book value of assets) or a market-based measure such as log market capitalisation. I use Log Assets as the primary specification because it is consistently available across both data sources, and include Log Market Cap as a robustness check. The limitation is acknowledged directly in the conclusions section.

Time-Varying Beta

The first version of this project assigned each firm a single Beta snapshot from yfinance — the same value for every year in the panel. This made Beta perfectly time-invariant, meaning the Fixed Effects estimator absorbed it entirely and it could not be estimated. In this version, Beta is computed annually by regressing each firm's 12 monthly returns against the S&P 500 (SPY) for that calendar year. This produces a genuinely time-varying risk measure and allows Beta to be estimated across all three model specifications.

Interpreting the DivYield Result in Pooled OLS

The large, highly significant DivYield coefficient in Pooled OLS (p=0.000) looks striking but is not a genuine finding. It disappears entirely in both Fixed and Random Effects. The explanation is straightforward: firms with low total assets tend to have higher asset-scaled dividend yields by construction, and firms with low total assets have lower log assets. Pooled OLS picks up this between-firm pattern and incorrectly attributes it to dividend policy. Once firm fixed effects are introduced — removing all time-invariant between-firm differences — the signal vanishes. This is a textbook illustration of why pooled OLS is inappropriate for panel data with firm heterogeneity.

Section 4

Hausman Test — Model Selection

The Hausman test examines whether the unobserved firm-level effects (management quality, business model, competitive position) are correlated with the explanatory variables. If they are, Random Effects produces biased estimates and Fixed Effects is required. The null hypothesis is that RE is consistent.

With p=0.6752 we fail to reject the null at any conventional significance level. Random Effects is therefore the consistent and more efficient estimator for this dataset. The high p-value also has an economic interpretation: firm-level unobservables are not systematically correlated with dividend policy choices, which is itself consistent with the MM view that dividend decisions are largely residual and do not reflect fundamental firm characteristics.

Market cap is a more theoretically appropriate valuation measure for a dividend relevance test, though it introduces the limitation of using a single current snapshot rather than a panel series.

Section 6

Limitations

A project like this should be judged partly on whether its author understands what it cannot claim. The following limitations are genuine, not performative.

Significant

Short time dimension. Five years (2020–2024) is a short panel. This limits the within-firm variation available to the Fixed and Random Effects estimators and makes results more sensitive to the particular economic conditions of the sample period — which happened to include COVID-19, a period of unusual dividend cuts and restorations that may not be representative of long-run behaviour. Ideally this study would use 10–15 years of data.

Significant

Log Assets is a size proxy, not a valuation measure. The MM proposition is about firm value, not firm size. Log Total Assets conflates the two. The robustness check with Log Market Cap partially addresses this but uses a cross-sectional snapshot rather than a panel series. A proper test would use time-varying Tobin's Q.

Moderate

Hybrid data sourcing. Using SimFin for 15 firms and yfinance for 10 introduces potential inconsistencies in how accounting items are labelled and adjusted, particularly for IFRS-reporting UK stocks. Results should be interpreted with this caveat in mind.

Minor

Sample selection. The 25 firms were chosen to represent large-cap value stocks. They are mature, consistently profitable dividend payers. The results may not generalise to growth firms, small-caps, or firms with irregular dividend histories — arguably the more interesting population for dividend relevance tests.

Section 7

Conclusion

Across all three panel specifications and the robustness check, neither the Dividend Payout Ratio nor Dividend Yield exerts a statistically significant effect on firm valuation. The Hausman test selects Random Effects as the preferred estimator, under which only ROE carries a significant coefficient. The apparently strong DivYield result in Pooled OLS is shown to be spurious — a product of between-firm heterogeneity rather than a genuine dividend signal.

These findings are consistent with the Modigliani-Miller dividend irrelevance proposition. For the large-cap value stocks in this sample, investors do not appear to systematically reward or penalise firms on the basis of dividend policy choices, conditional on profitability and systematic risk.

Whether this conclusion would hold with a longer time series, a broader sample, or a superior valuation proxy such as Tobin's Q remains an open question — and the most honest summary of what this project can and cannot claim.

Key Takeaway

Dividend policy does not significantly predict firm valuation within this panel. The result is robust across Fixed Effects, Random Effects, and a market cap robustness check. The Modigliani-Miller irrelevance proposition is supported, with the caveat that a five-year panel of large-cap stocks is not an ideal testing ground for a proposition that assumes frictionless markets and long-run equilibrium.
